## SECTION 8 — ESCALATION & EMERGENCY

**Emergency → 911 IMMEDIATELY — DO NOT DRIVE:**
- Chest pain or pressure radiating to arm, jaw, or back (possible heart attack)
- Sudden shortness of breath at rest
- Loss of consciousness or near-fainting
- Rapid pounding irregular heartbeat with dizziness or chest pain
- Pacemaker or ICD shock firing
- Sudden severe leg swelling with shortness of breath (possible pulmonary embolism)
- Signs of stroke: facial drooping, arm weakness, slurred speech, sudden confusion

**Urgent same-day (call clinic first):**
- New onset AFib or known AFib with rapid rate and palpitations without chest pain
- Worsening leg edema in known heart failure patient
- Blood pressure consistently above 180/110 despite medications
- New concerning EKG from another provider (fax to us and call immediately)

**Escalation contact:** Cardiac nurse — (512) 555-1002 | 24-hour answering service: (512) 555-1000

## SECTION 9 — TOP FAQs

**Q1: Do I need a referral to see a cardiologist?**
A1: For PPO plans, you can self-refer. HMO plans typically require a referral from your primary care physician. We recommend calling your insurance to confirm. We can also see you for an out-of-pocket consultation without referral.

**Q2: What should I do if I have chest pain right now?**
A2: Call 911 immediately. Do not drive yourself to the clinic or any office — chest pain requires emergency evaluation. If you have nitroglycerin prescribed, use it as directed while waiting for help.

**Q3: What is an echocardiogram and does it hurt?**
A3: An echocardiogram is an ultrasound of your heart — it's painless and takes about 45 minutes. It shows us the structure and function of your heart valves and chambers. No radiation or needles involved.

**Q4: What is a stress test and how do I prepare?**
A4: A stress test monitors your heart during exercise (treadmill). You must fast for at least 4 hours before. Wear comfortable shoes. If you cannot walk on a treadmill, a pharmacologic stress test (medication-induced) is available. Book an early morning slot.

**Q5: My doctor said I have AFib — what does that mean?**
A5: Atrial fibrillation is an irregular heart rhythm that increases stroke risk. Dr. Okonkwo specializes in AFib management including rate control, rhythm control medications, and blood thinners to prevent stroke. Book a consultation — bring your EKG report.

**Q6: I have a pacemaker — can you manage it here?**
A6: Yes. We have a device clinic for pacemaker and ICD check-ups. Remote monitoring is also available. Book a device check appointment and bring your pacemaker ID card.

**Q7: What is the difference between a nuclear stress test and an echo stress test?**
A7: Both evaluate blood flow to your heart during stress. A nuclear test uses a radioactive tracer + gamma camera and is more sensitive for detecting blockages. An echo stress test uses ultrasound. Dr. Okonkwo will recommend the right test based on your symptoms and history.

**Q8: Can I stop taking my blood pressure medications before my appointment?**
A8: Do NOT stop any heart medications without talking to your doctor first. However, for stress testing, beta-blockers may need to be held — we will give you specific instructions when you book your stress test.

**Q9: I was told I have a heart murmur — is that serious?**
A9: Many murmurs are harmless (functional murmurs). Some indicate valve disease that needs monitoring or treatment. An echocardiogram can tell us the cause. Book a consultation with Dr. Okonkwo.

**Q10: How do I know if I'm at risk for a heart attack?**
A10: Key risk factors include high blood pressure, high cholesterol, diabetes, smoking, family history, obesity, and age. Dr. Okonkwo offers comprehensive cardiovascular risk assessments. Book a preventive cardiology visit.
