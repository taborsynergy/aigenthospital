## SECTION 8 — ESCALATION & EMERGENCY

**Emergency → 911 IMMEDIATELY:**
- Active seizure lasting more than 5 minutes (status epilepticus)
- First-ever seizure (go to ER, not neurology clinic)
- Signs of stroke: sudden face drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty, sudden severe headache ("worst headache of my life"), sudden vision loss
- Sudden loss of consciousness
- Rapidly progressive weakness or paralysis (possible Guillain-Barré)
- Sudden altered mental status / confusion in an elderly patient

**Urgent same-day (call clinic):**
- Breakthrough seizure in a known epilepsy patient (especially if driving or at height)
- MS relapse: new or worsening neurological symptoms lasting more than 24 hours
- New severe headache different from prior migraines (rule out bleed or meningitis)
- Post-lumbar puncture: severe positional headache (post-LP headache)

**Escalation contact:** Neurology nurse — (512) 555-1102 | After-hours answering service: (512) 555-1100

**After-hours:** "For a stroke or first-time seizure, call 911 immediately. For ongoing seizures (known epilepsy), follow your seizure action plan and call 911 if the seizure does not stop within 5 minutes. For urgent neurological concerns, our answering service at (512) 555-1100 can reach the on-call neurologist. For non-urgent matters, leave a message and we'll call back next business day."

## SECTION 9 — TOP FAQs

**Q1: How long will I wait for a new patient appointment?**
A1: New patient slots are typically available 2–4 weeks out. If you have a recent seizure, suspected MS, or other urgent neurological concern, call us directly — we hold urgent slots for time-sensitive conditions.

**Q2: I had a seizure last night — what should I do?**
A2: If it was your first seizure ever, you should have gone to the ER — go now if you haven't. After a first seizure, the ER refers you to neurology for follow-up within 1–2 weeks. If you are a known epilepsy patient and had a breakthrough seizure, call us today for an urgent appointment.

**Q3: Do I need a referral to see a neurologist?**
A3: PPO plans allow self-referral. HMO plans require a referral from your primary care doctor. We recommend calling your insurance first. We also see self-pay patients without referral.

**Q4: What is Botox for migraines and does insurance cover it?**
A4: Botox is FDA-approved for chronic migraine (15+ headache days per month). It is injected into 31 sites on the head and neck every 12 weeks. Most major insurance plans cover it after failing 2 oral migraine prevention medications. Dr. Vasquez will help document medical necessity.

**Q5: What is an EMG test and does it hurt?**
A5: An EMG (electromyography) measures electrical activity in muscles and nerves. It involves small needle electrodes placed in muscles — you will feel brief discomfort similar to a blood draw. Most patients tolerate it well. The test takes 45–60 minutes and helps diagnose nerve damage, carpal tunnel, and neuromuscular conditions.

**Q6: My mother has been getting more forgetful — is that Alzheimer's?**
A6: Memory changes can have many causes — some reversible (thyroid, vitamin B12, sleep disorders, medications) and some progressive (Alzheimer's, Lewy body dementia). Dr. Vasquez will perform a full cognitive evaluation including MoCA, blood work, and MRI referral. Book a memory evaluation.

**Q7: I was just diagnosed with MS — what happens now?**
A7: Multiple sclerosis is manageable with the right disease-modifying therapy (DMT). Dr. Vasquez specializes in MS and will review your MRI, symptoms, and history to recommend the best treatment. Early treatment significantly reduces long-term disability. Book an MS consultation.

**Q8: Can you treat Parkinson's disease?**
A8: Yes. Dr. Vasquez manages Parkinson's disease and movement disorders with medication optimization (levodopa, dopamine agonists) and referrals to physical/occupational therapy. For advanced Parkinson's (DBS evaluation), we coordinate with a movement disorder specialist.

**Q9: I get migraines 3 times a week — what are my options?**
A9: Chronic migraine (15+ days per month) has several treatment pathways: daily preventive medications (topiramate, amitriptyline, beta-blockers), monthly CGRP biologics (Aimovig, Ajovy, Emgality), and Botox every 12 weeks. Book a headache consultation and we'll create a personalized plan.

**Q10: My child had a febrile seizure — is that epilepsy?**
A10: Febrile seizures (seizures with fever) are common in young children (6 months–5 years) and are usually not epilepsy. However, they should always be evaluated. We see patients 10 years and older; for younger children, a pediatric neurologist is recommended. We can provide a referral.
