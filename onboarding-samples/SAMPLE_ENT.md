## SECTION 8 — ESCALATION & EMERGENCY

**Emergency → 911 immediately:**
- Airway obstruction or stridor (noisy, strained breathing)
- Sudden inability to swallow with throat swelling (possible epiglottitis or anaphylaxis)
- Suspected ingested or inhaled foreign object in child causing breathing difficulty
- Uncontrolled nosebleed lasting 30+ minutes that does not respond to pressure
- Rapidly spreading neck swelling with fever (possible Ludwig's angina or deep neck infection)

**Urgent same-day (call clinic):**
- Sudden hearing loss in one or both ears (sudden sensorineural hearing loss — MEDICAL EMERGENCY, treat within 72 hours)
- Severe vertigo with inability to stand/walk
- Nosebleed not controlled after 20 minutes of direct pressure
- Ear foreign body (especially in a child)

**Escalation contact:** ENT nurse on duty — (512) 555-0808

**After-hours:** "Our office is closed. For breathing difficulties, severe throat swelling, or airway concerns, call 911 immediately. For sudden hearing loss (a medical emergency), go to the nearest ER. For nosebleeds, pinch your nose firmly for 20 minutes. For all other concerns, call back during office hours or visit capitolent.com."

## SECTION 9 — TOP FAQs

**Q1: My child keeps getting ear infections — do they need ear tubes?**
A1: Ear tubes may be considered if a child has had 3 or more ear infections in 6 months, or persistent fluid in the ear causing hearing loss. Dr. Osei will evaluate and recommend based on your child's history. Book a pediatric ENT consultation.

**Q2: I have a constant ringing in my ears — can you help?**
A2: Yes. Tinnitus (ringing in the ears) can be caused by hearing loss, noise exposure, or other conditions. Dr. Walsh will perform a hearing evaluation and Dr. Osei will assess the cause. Book a tinnitus evaluation.

**Q3: How do I know if I need a septoplasty?**
A3: If you have a deviated septum causing nasal obstruction, difficulty breathing through one or both nostrils, or chronic sinus issues, you may be a candidate. We evaluate with an in-office nasal endoscopy. Book a sinus consultation.

**Q4: What is balloon sinuplasty and is it painful?**
A4: Balloon sinuplasty is a minimally invasive in-office procedure to open blocked sinus passages. Most patients tolerate it with local anesthetic and feel pressure, not pain. Recovery is 1–2 days vs weeks for traditional sinus surgery.

**Q5: Do you treat sleep apnea?**
A5: We evaluate patients who are not candidates for or who cannot tolerate CPAP. Options include surgical procedures (tonsillectomy, soft palate work) or Inspire upper airway stimulation implant for qualifying patients. Book a sleep apnea consultation.

**Q6: Can you test my child for food allergies?**
A6: We do environmental and some food allergy panels. For comprehensive pediatric food allergy management, we may co-manage with a pediatric allergist. Book an allergy consultation and we'll discuss what's best for your child.

**Q7: How long does allergy immunotherapy (allergy shots) take?**
A7: The buildup phase takes about 6–8 months (shots weekly). The maintenance phase is monthly injections for 3–5 years. Many patients see significant relief in year 1. Sublingual (under-tongue) drops are available for those who prefer to avoid injections.

**Q8: I have swollen lymph nodes in my neck for 3 weeks — is that serious?**
A8: Neck masses lasting more than 2–3 weeks should always be evaluated by an ENT. While most are benign (infection-related), Dr. Osei will assess for thyroid, salivary gland, or lymph node conditions. Book an urgent evaluation.

**Q9: Can you remove earwax? How does it work?**
A9: Yes — cerumen (earwax) removal is a quick in-office procedure using gentle irrigation or suction. It takes about 15 minutes. Cost is $75 self-pay and covered by most insurance plans.

**Q10: My child has a peanut in their ear — what should I do?**
A10: Do NOT try to remove it at home — this can push it deeper. Call us immediately for a same-day appointment. If the child is in distress or unable to breathe, call 911.
